    \subsection*{Ancient Greek: Reflexive Pronouns in Third Person}
    \begin{itemize}[label={}, leftmargin=0]
        \item Kristoffer E.D.\ Paulsson (2026), Independent Scholar.
    \end{itemize}
    The third-person reflexive pronouns (\grc{ἑαυτοῦ}, \grc{-ῆς}, \grc{-οῦ}) exhibit full gender marking
    (masculine, feminine, neuter) because their referents may be any nominal, including inanimate or non-personal
    entities.
    By contrast, first- and second-person reflexives (\grc{ἐμαυτοῦ}/\grc{ἐμαυτῆς}, \grc{σεαυτοῦ}/\grc{σεαυτῆς})
    have only masculine and feminine forms and lack a neuter, since speakers and addressees are inherently animate and
    thus never neuter.
    This asymmetry is pragmatic rather than morphological, aligning with the genderless nature of
    the personal pronouns (\grc{ἐγώ}, \grc{σύ}) and their compounding with the gendered forms of \grc{αὐτός}.

    The third-person reflexive pronouns are formed by compounding the personal pronoun \grc{ἑ}, in the third-person
    accusative singular, with the corresponding oblique cases of the intensive pronoun \grc{αὐτός}.
    For example, combining \grc{ἑ} with \grc{αὐτοῦ} yields \grc{ἑαυτοῦ}; this formation applies across cases,
    numbers, and genders.
